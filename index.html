Types of Entrepreneurs ★★

By Behaviour:

  • Innovative – introduces new products/processes (Elon Musk)
  • Imitative – copies & adapts successful ideas
  • Fabian – very cautious, changes only when forced
  • Drone – refuses to change even at a loss
  • Social – solves social problems

By Motivation:

  • Pure – motivated by profit
  • Induced – motivated by govt incentives (most suitable for India)
  • Motivated – self-actualization driven

Most suitable for India:

Induced + Agricultural — rural economy, govt schemes (Mudra, PMEGP), ground-level problems

Maslow's Need Hierarchy ★★★

5 levels (bottom to top):

  • 1. Physiological – food, shelter, clothing (basic survival)
  • 2. Safety – job security, financial stability
  • 3. Social/Belonging – friendship, community
  • 4. Esteem – recognition, status, respect
  • 5. Self-Actualization – realize full potential

Entrepreneurs driven by esteem + self-actualization once lower needs met. Rural entrepreneurs → physiological/safety; Tech founders → esteem/self-actualization.

McClelland's Achievement Theory ★★★

Needs are learned through experience, not innate (1961). Three needs:

  • nAch (Need for Achievement) – excel, solve challenges, personal responsibility. Most critical for entrepreneurship. High-nAch → more likely to start businesses.
  • nPow (Need for Power) – influence & lead others
  • nAff (Need for Affiliation) – social acceptance. Usually lower in successful entrepreneurs.

EDPs designed to develop nAch. Societies with high nAch show faster economic growth.

Other Motivation Theories

ERG Theory (Alderfer):

  • Existence (physiological + safety)
  • Relatedness (social + esteem)
  • Growth (self-actualization)

Multiple needs active simultaneously. Frustration at higher level → regression to lower.

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory:

  • Hygiene factors (salary, security) – absence causes dissatisfaction, presence doesn't motivate
  • Motivators (achievement, recognition, growth) – genuinely motivate → aligns with entrepreneurship

EDP — Entrepreneurial Development Programme ★★

Purpose: Develop entrepreneurial competencies — knowledge, skills, motivation.

Objectives:

  • Identify latent talent; develop managerial + technical skills
  • Create awareness of govt schemes; promote self-employment

Phases:

  • Pre-training – publicity, selection, infrastructure prep
  • Training – classroom inputs, factory visits, entrepreneur interactions
  • Post-training – follow-up, loan assistance, monitoring

Key agencies: NIESBUD, NSIC, DICs, commercial banks

Cooperative Society ★★★

Characteristics:

  • Voluntary membership; democratic control (1 member = 1 vote)
  • Service motive (not profit); limited return on capital
  • Surplus distributed proportional to transactions
  • Separate legal entity; govt support/subsidies

Types:

  • Consumer – bulk buy & sell to members (Kendriya Bhandar)
  • Producer – collective purchase of raw materials (Handloom coops)
  • Credit – loans at low interest (Urban coop banks)
  • Marketing – better market access (AMUL)
  • Farming / Housing – joint cultivation / housing

NPV Method ★★★ Every Paper — Numericals!

NPV = Σ [Ct / (1+r)^t] − C0

Decision Rule: NPV > 0 → Accept | NPV < 0 → Reject

Example (memorise this):

Investment ₹1,00,000 | Cash inflow ₹40,000/yr for 3 yrs | Rate 10%

YearCFPV Factor @10%PV
140,0000.90936,360
240,0000.82633,040
340,0000.75130,040
Total PV99,440

NPV = 99,440 − 1,00,000 = −₹560 → Reject

IRR Method ★★★

IRR = discount rate where NPV = 0. Represents project's own rate of return.

IRR = r1 + [NPV1 / (NPV1 − NPV2)] × (r2 − r1)

Decision: IRR > Cost of Capital → Accept | IRR < Cost of Capital → Reject

Using same example: NPV @9% = +₹1,240 | NPV @10% = −₹560

IRR = 9 + [1240/(1240+560)] × 1 = 9.69% → Reject (less than 10%)

Other Evaluation Methods ★★

Payback Period:

PP = Initial Investment / Annual Cash Inflow

Accept if PP < max acceptable period. Simple but ignores time value & post-payback flows.

Break-Even Analysis:

BEP (units) = Fixed Costs / (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)

Profitability Index (PI):

PI = PV of Cash Inflows / Initial Investment   (Accept if PI > 1)

Project Appraisal Stages ★★★

6 stages — each 1 mark in a 6-mark question:

  • Technical – technology, capacity, machinery, raw material, location
  • Financial – cost, funding, P&L projections, cash flow, DSCR, BEP
  • Market – demand, competition, pricing, distribution
  • Economic/Social – employment, import substitution, regional impact
  • Environmental – EIA, pollution compliance, waste disposal
  • Managerial – promoter background, team quality, organisational structure

Working Capital ★★★

Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Types:

  • Gross WC – Total current assets
  • Net WC – Current assets minus current liabilities (positive = healthy)
  • Permanent WC – Minimum always needed for basic operations
  • Temporary WC – Extra needed during peak seasons

Importance (7 points):

Smooth daily operations Production continuity Meets short-term obligations Supports growth Financial cushion Enhances creditworthiness Measures liquidity

Break-Even Analysis ★★★

Contribution = Sales − Variable Cost P/V Ratio = Contribution ÷ Sales BEP = Fixed Cost ÷ P/V Ratio Margin of Safety = Actual Sales − BEP Sales MoS % = (Actual − BEP) ÷ Actual × 100

Example (memorise):

SP=₹10, VC=₹6, FC=₹400 → Contribution=₹4 → P/V=40% → BEP=₹400÷0.4=₹1,000 (100 units)

How it helps:

  • Profitability – knows exactly how much each unit contributes
  • Risk – high MoS = low risk; low MoS = vulnerable
  • Pricing decisions – tests impact of price changes
  • Target profit – Sales = (FC + Desired Profit) ÷ P/V Ratio
